The shift didn't happen overnight. It started with weekend visits. My adoptive parents had a house about ten minutes away from Mummy's place, and as I mentioned, my dad was the first to move back to Ghana.

The new setup included a house help and Joy — the son of one of my dad's friends who was living there too. Joy was older, a cool guy, and he had two older siblings I really liked. Every Saturday, my dad and Joy would drive over to pick me up. We'd head back to their place and do... whatever it was we did. Honestly, I can't even remember because it definitely wasn't as fun as what I was used to.

Then came Sundays. Sunday meant church, and church at my dad's was a whole different world. Because my dad had been away from Ghana, traveling the world for years before coming back, everyone knew him. And this church had rules. So. Many. Rules. At Mummy's (my auntie), I was used to just being a kid. Here? Absolutely no jeans, no shirts, no sneakers. Nothing. It had to be a strict shirt-and-tie combo. Bruh. I spent the entire day counting down the hours until Sunday evening when I could finally escape back to my auntie's.

Through all of this, I was still going to Danem. It was one of my constants which I thought wasn't going to change.

But between 2008 and 2009, the temporary weekend arrangement became the official timeline. The day finally came. They packed up everything. Everything. And just like that, I had officially moved. I was living with my dad in this massive, quiet house with the house help. The day Mummy and my cousins dropped me off and turned around to leave... man, I cried so bad.

Welcome to the new house. New people, and an endless list of new rules. No TV on weekdays. Prayer meetings on Wednesday evenings. All-night services on Fridays. Even my school routine flipped — I started taking the school bus. No more morning car rides with Mummy, no more stopping for snacks or emergency stationery replacements. Nobody was stopping anywhere for me anymore. In fact, to this day, I have never had a single birthday cake with my own name written on it.

As I said — I want an all good, all holy child. I had my own devils and problems growing up. I started stealing small amounts of money here and there, just to buy ice cream or little things to comfort myself. Naturally, I got caught and punished a lot. It took a good amount of time and self-reflection to finally stop doing it.

Eventually, things became manageable. I was essentially an only child in that big house. Looking around, there were no pictures of me as a baby, or photos of me with them. Back then, I didn't know a single thing about being adopted. Whenever people asked, I just told them my parents gave birth to me but had to travel overseas, so I stayed with my auntie. It was the only story that made sense to me.

There was one upside to the new regime, though. My dad was always on me about academics, constantly teaching me math. Because of that heavy focus, I actually turned out to be really good at it, and eventually, I genuinely started to like it. I became a pretty good student.

And thankfully, my tie to my childhood home wasn't completely severed. I was allowed to go back to Mummy's place for occasional weekends or holidays. The second I stepped through her door, that familiar wave of happiness would hit me all over again.

I slowly adjusted to this new rhythm, taking it one day at a time, until the next big shift arrived in 2009 — the year my mother was scheduled to touch down next.
