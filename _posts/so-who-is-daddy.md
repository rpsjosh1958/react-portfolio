My dad is a doctor — an educational doctor, not a medical doctor. I think he's a professor right now; he's passed the doctor stage. As I mentioned earlier, he is also a ship captain who inspects ships and handles all that. And on top of that, he's a pastor. Yeah, my dad is a pastor.

But let's talk about the father side of him. Well, he's not your regular father. That's all I'll say.

I feel like he handles his responsibilities as the man of the house most of the time, as he should. But sometimes I feel like he wants us to constantly appreciate him just for doing his basic responsibility, which in my case doesn't make any sense. I'm sorry, but it really doesn't. If you have to do something, you do it because you have to. It's just like how he always tells me that it's my responsibility as the kid to do stuff in the house, clean up, and handle chores. So I don't really know why he is how he is. But then again, I do appreciate him.

I feel that in his own way, or based on how he grew up, he thinks he's doing the right thing by providing for his family. I respect him for that, but if I were to list the top ten reasons why I like him, providing wouldn't be on it. The only real reason I am grateful for him is for teaching me the Word of God. Honestly, he's taught me a lot through our Bible devotions. I genuinely like the way he understands scriptures and is able to explain them to me. When I'm talking to friends who need help, I use exactly what he taught me — referencing the Bible, explaining who Jesus is and what He's done, and linking real-life situations back to scripture to navigate life. It's all through what I learned from him, and for that, I'm grateful.

I used to think he was all jovial and stuff, but I eventually realized it wasn't towards me. I feel like he prefers other people — he calls them his children, his sons, his daughters — and the way he treats them is not the same way he treats me. To be honest, I feel like an outcast. Like it's always *'his son'* in a detached kind of way.

Honestly, I needed more than that. I needed a father where I could just come home from work and tell him, "Oh, this and that happened," and have a real discussion. I wanted to talk about what happened at work, or even just football — come on! I wanted to have those normal "boys-boys" discussions. It would have made me feel closer to him, letting me feel like I could tell him anything because he's a man who is experienced and might understand and help me out. But I've never felt that way towards him. As a kid, I used to be terrified to talk to him because he was so strict. You didn't even know what he did for fun, and you couldn't tell if he was even approachable.

Because of that, I grew up without that kind of relationship with him. Yet funny enough, I easily had that exact relationship with his brothers, my other uncles, and my aunties. I can tell them anything and everything — it's just easier. But for my own dad, it's not.

One time, we were traveling to a ship — just me and him — and I had my phone out watching football highlights. This man straight up told me that I was wasting my time and that I should be doing something productive instead. I was like, *"What?"* This is a man who buys DSTV subscriptions just to watch football all day on Saturdays. The journey from Accra to Takoradi was about five to six hours long, and you're telling me I'm not being productive?

I don't know how it'll ever change. As I said, he took us on vacation once, and I think that was a time where things were kind of cool. I won't say calm, because he's always been strict — like when he was teaching me math — he's just been strict throughout my entire life. Sometimes I was even scared to have fun as a child because I never knew what he might like or dislike, so I mostly stayed cooped up in my room doing my own thing. And even then, if he found out, it usually turned into a problem.

When I was a kid, I used to visit my friend Charles a lot. Our parents knew each other, we're good friends. I mostly went to his house because he was allowed to be more "childish" than me. I don't mean acting like a baby, but he did normal things that children do in their homes — watching cartoons, riding bicycles, playing football. I barely ever got to do that at home. Once the house help left, I was mostly working around the house. If I got to play at all, it was only for a short time. I didn't have any video game consoles growing up. It was just me, bro.

I'm not even playing — sometimes I felt really jealous of people who had siblings, at least they had each other. As I said in previous chapters, we had people come live with us and eventually I felt like I had siblings. But now where are they? My parents had problems with all of them. Some valid, some not. Some also decided to just leave. Funny enough, I don't even think my dad or mom know I'm still in contact with most of them. There are days I meet them and they say, *"Wow, you're still in that house?"* And it hurts sometimes. For a place I wanna call home and feel like it's my safe place, people actually see it as a prison. Uptight rules, restrictions, no freedom. And some of these comments come from people close to my dad or mom. I think my dad is someone you love from a distance. If you don't live with him it's actually better.

Because my house was like that, I visited Charles constantly, which eventually became a problem because it was always me going to his house and he never came to mine. But I understood why my friends wouldn't come see me — my parents were too strict, and there was absolutely nothing fun to do in our house. Like, nothing, bro.

One thing my father always says is that he grew up alone, so he's used to it. Apparently, his mom died when he was just a kid, so he felt alone. I don't know if that means he wants me to grow up alone without people around me too, but it's a weird attitude that I don't want to adopt. Growing up, I just had to tolerate his behavior. As a kid, I was scared because he was the authority, and he would discipline me for any wrong I did — I wouldn't argue that. But even when he was clearly wrong, it was always one of those "your father is always right, you are always the wrong one" situations. He would never apologize. I didn't really understand that dynamic growing up because I used to get pissed off easily. And I think that really affected me. As I said, I didn't have friends visiting, so if you see me being alone most of the time now, it's because I was conditioned to be alone a long time ago.

But it's not like I'm actually an introvert. I really like going out, talking to people, and being around people, but I've simply learned how to be on my own because I was always left on my own. I learned not to offend people, not to rely on people, and to just keep to myself.

As I grew older, I realized that if I were to follow in his footsteps, I would have serious problems. He was never my inspiration, my idol, or my mentor. Even as a kid, I wasn't blind — I could see exactly what was wrong and what was right. I never had a formal mentor growing up, and I never tried to look for one either. I was mostly just by myself, so if I wanted to do anything, I just prayed about it and went my own way.

As I mentioned before, he didn't even bother to properly check me in when I went to uni — he just dropped me off at the hostel and left. It was actually during my university days that a friend told me about his own turning point. He called his dad and gave him a piece of his mind, telling him he wasn't a kid anymore. Back then, hearing that while I was still in school, I thought, *"Nah, I can't do that."* Even if I finished school, I'd still have to go back to his house. If I had spoken my mind to them over the phone back then, I'd be dead.

My upkeep money in uni was only about 300 cedis a month, which wasn't good at all. That's why I had to start my side business to get extra cash. But there was one specific thing he did back then that still pisses me off every single time I think about it. My Auntie Joyce gave him 1,000 Cedis for a birthday event, intending for it to be distributed to her nephews and nieces. When I found out about the money, I thought, *"Oh nice, he's going to send the whole 1,000 to me."* Uni was tough. I was dead broke. Instead, he called me and casually mentioned that Auntie Joyce had sent something. I called her to thank her, but to this day, I am certain that he just held onto that 1,000 cedis and used it to pay my regular 300 cedi monthly upkeep. I'm not even going to lie to you, I just knew. And I was so incredibly broke at that time. But anyway, it is what it is.

Even while I was away at university, this man would call me from Accra all the way to Kumasi just to ask when I was coming home because the grass needed to be mowed or some chore needed to be done. They would call me across the country to tell me the grass was overgrown and I needed to come, completely ignoring that I had classes, lectures, and exams. One particular weekend, he put serious pressure on me to come down. Since he controlled my upkeep money, I had no choice. I had to sit in a car for a six-hour journey just to handle the lawn. I came down on a Friday or Saturday, spent the whole weekend cleaning the house, mowing the grass, doing laundry and ironing, went to church on Sunday, and had to travel right back to Kumasi on Monday morning. Can you imagine? Traveling all that way just to clean his house and go back. I really feel like a houseboy more than a son.

When I told my auntie about it, she was completely shocked. *"Woah. You left school just to come do this?"* Anytime I think about that situation, I literally want to cry.

One day at home, about a month into my National Service, I asked my dad if he could help me get a new laptop, or if he could just give me one of his. The man literally had two or three laptops sitting around — one he used constantly, one that seemed to be a backup, and another where I didn't even know what was wrong with it. When I asked him, he looked at me and told me that since I was starting National Service and would be working, I should just earn my own money and buy myself one.

I'm not going to lie to you, that completely broke my heart. He said it so normally, so jovially, and it just crushed me. I wanted to cry right then and there. It was from that exact moment that I made a firm decision: I am never asking them for anything ever again.

When I started my NSS, I didn't ask them for transport money, I didn't ask them for a single thing. Nothing, like, zero. Service was incredibly hard — the monthly allowance was only about 1,000 Cedis and I had to pay transport and feed myself — but I started pulling in side jobs and design gigs, so after a while things became fairly okay. Eventually, my auntie bought me a laptop, and that was when the actual web development journey truly took off.

The funny thing is, right before I got that laptop, I got robbed at a bus stop close to work while coming home at night, and they stole my phone. But this post isn't about that.

I got my new laptop and kept progressing in web design and graphics. After that, I managed to buy myself an Xbox console from my own money. This man somehow found out, came to my room, and started questioning me: *"Where did you get this from? How much was it?"* I told him it cost 2,000 Cedis. He looked at it and said, *"Wow. So you can use 2,000 Cedis just to buy this?"* He was deeply bothered by the fact that I didn't view him as a father to ask him for things anymore. You could see he was genuinely hurt. He even went to complain to one of our family friends, asking them to come and talk to me about my attitude.

I was so incredibly pissed off, bro. I asked you for a tool to help me work, and you refused. If I find a way to hustle, make my own money, and buy things for myself, why the hell are you angry? Make it make sense — how am I the wrong person in this situation?

The ultimate irony? Now that I am working and out of National Service, he suddenly wants me to design flyers for his seminars and workshops. Just recently, he told me I should design a business card for him. And every single time I think about it, I'm just like, *"Bro, when I asked you for a laptop to start my career, what did you tell me?"*

During National Service, it was my auntie who came through for me. When I landed my first actual remote tech job after service, it wasn't him either — it was my cousin, my auntie's daughter, whose friend was a CEO of a software company. She put me on, I completed the assessment and interview, and I started working. And even working a remote job in that house was hectic because they would call me up and down constantly to do this and do that. I was so completely, thoroughly annoyed.

He has completely stopped talking to his own siblings because of family issues. He only communicates with his younger brother. Outside of that, he doesn't speak to his family, and he doesn't talk to my mom's side of the family either, except for my grandmother. He barely gets any visitors at the house. Sometimes I honestly wonder if the man even has friends. He holds onto things way too much and he is far too petty — he never forgets anything you've ever done to him, and if something hurts him, he carries it forever.

But growing up through it, I'm not scared anymore. I just prefer to keep my distance. Our interactions don't last more than a minute. It's just a quick "do this, do that" and I walk away.

The breaking point finally came while I was working my remote job after National Service. I was completely fed up, and I was giving off a visible attitude in the house — just waking up, ignoring things, and going out. One night, I came home and he told me to sit down in the hall.

To give you context on the internet situation in the house: my dad had gotten fiber internet, but he kept the router entirely in his study. Only he used it, only he knew the password. Since I was working remotely, there were days I was home alone and desperate for a connection, so I found a way to get the password. Admittedly, I overused it — connecting my console, my laptop, downloading games — and he eventually found out. It turned into a massive issue, which pushed me to just buy my own Starlink so I wouldn't have to deal with their rules. I put it on the roof, ran the wires down to my room, and eventually he saw it.

So that night in the hall, he looked at me and asked, *"Do you even like staying here?"* I didn't answer at first. He asked again, *"The device you bought, what is it?"* I told him straight: *"It's Starlink internet. I bought it for my work."* He started flaming up — *"Why didn't you inform me? Don't you want to stay here anymore? This is my house."*

Calmly, I looked at him and said, *"Can I speak the truth?"* And I just let it all out. I told him everything that had been building up for years. I told him: *"I need help in this house. I can't be doing every single thing by myself anymore. I am growing up, I have my own responsibilities, and I have absolutely no time for myself. It is always my job, housework, church work, housework, church work, and nobody cares if I am exhausted. All you know is that I am the kid, so I have to just keep doing and doing. I can't even approach you to talk about anything."*

He got incredibly, violently mad — to the point where he almost kicked me out of the house, and honestly, I was ready to leave. I was crying, furious that I was finally opening up about my struggles and his only reaction was to get angry at me. It became a massive family issue. You know what everyone told me? *"That's how he is. You just have to manage him."* Huh??

I've been through a lot with this man. At first, I used to think it was punishment because I was a difficult boy when I was younger. But as I grew up, I noticed other people in our extended family who were also adopted, and the way they are treated by their adoptive parents is completely different. You wouldn't even know they were adopted because they are so close. One of them is only two years older than me, and his mother bought him a car, helped him secure a job, and trusts him completely with the house when she travels. And I'm just looking at that like, *"Bro, if I had received that kind of love, do you think I'd turn out the way I am now?"*

There have been times, I'm not going to lie, where I've told myself I am glad he is not my biological dad. Because if I turned out like him, I don't think I'd like myself at all.

Growing up without an anchor or any real help in that house forced me to figure things out on my own. As a kid, I thought it was just my cross to bear. It was a brutal way to grow up, but it forced me to break free, pivot into tech, and build a weird life entirely on my own terms.

So eventually people told me to apologize to him, because *"he's your father, blah blah blah."*

There might be more to say, but for now, this is just my idea of him.

I don't say this so you hate him or anything. As I said — if you do know my dad and you like him, that's very fine. I can see that side of him.

Maybe I'm the problem.

I'm just 24 writing this. Maybe i don't understand. Maybe I might understand in future and it'll make sense. But for now...
