If I ever need to find a happy place in my mind, I always go straight back to my childhood in that house. The love I got there was absolute. In fact, I didn't just have one mother — I had three.

Between Mummy (my auntie), Ayesha (the older sister who was always advising me and sometimes taking me to school when both parents weren't around), and Stephanie (the younger sister, who was my buddy buddy — following her around the neighbourhood and our walks, meeting her other friends with younger siblings), I was thoroughly looked after. I used to follow the girls everywhere. And then there was my uncle. He passed away some years back, but I'll never forget playing football with him in the house, or the days he'd drive me to school when Mummy couldn't.

Speaking of my uncle, I've actually never told anyone this before: as a kid, I absolutely hated kenkey and pepper with fish. Hated it. But whenever Mummy made it for my uncle and we sat down to eat with him, his portion always tasted incredible. I don't know if he had some secret spice or if food just tastes better when it belongs to your uncle, but I'd happily eat it with him. (And just to be clear, Mummy is the absolute best cook, no lies told. Our house ran on a strict food timetable, and the days jollof came up on the rotation were legendary. Even banku and okro soup.)

My favorite daily ritual was waiting for Mummy to get home from work. The second her car pulled into the garage, I was the first one out the door to open her car door, greet her, and carry her things. And she always brought something back for me — usually chocolate, even Junior Graphic, because she used to work at Graphic.

One night, I tried to stay up for her but ended up crashing out. In the middle of the night, I was woken up because they had a surprise for me. Right there, late in the night, was my very first bicycle. I was so insanely excited I couldn't even go back to sleep.

Now, let's be honest: I wasn't some perfectly behaved, holy kid. I had my fair share of mischief. I distinctly remember stealing chewing gum from my uncle's car right before he dropped me off at school. Another time, I was outside trying to use a cutlass to remove some weeds, and accidentally burst a water pipe. Yeah... I was busted.

While all this beautiful chaos was happening, my biological parents were still in the US. At the time, I didn't really know them. We'd speak on the phone, but to me, they were "Uncle T" and "Auntie BB." Whenever Mummy traveled overseas and came back, she'd bring tons of toys and clothes, saying, "Your mother said I should give this to you." I'd jump on the phone, say thank you, and go right back to playing. Life was good. I was a kid, and I was happy.

But then, the blueprint started to change.

My dad was the first to move back to Ghana. Suddenly, the plan was put into motion: I had to start transitioning back to the parents who had legally adopted me. It started with weekend visits to my dad. To be entirely honest, it was weird. There were days I would literally cry because I just didn't want to leave the comfort of the only home I really knew. But the process had begun, and I was slowly being prepared to pack up my childhood and move in with them for good.
