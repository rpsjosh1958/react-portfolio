The next part of my story is based on my mother, Barbara Naa Odey Tetteh. Honestly, after writing all that stuff about my dad, I didn't even know what to say or where to start with her. She is the eldest of her siblings on my grandmother's side, and her younger sister happens to be my aunt, my favorite aunt — Rosemary. I've always known my mom as a baker and a caterer, but before all of that, I heard she was an administrator at Mauritania Hospital in the US. They had a whole life and career there before they came back to Ghana.

But let's talk about the mother side of her. With my mother, I have these deeply divided, double-sided emotions. There are days where she is nice and there are days where she is not. Days where she just doesn't care at all, and days where she actually does what a mother does. On her good days, she'll randomly give me money or do something nice, especially when I don't even ask for it. She's a great cook because of her catering background and knows exactly what she's doing in the kitchen. Even when she cooks things I absolutely hate eating, I have no option but to eat them. Once you get close to her on a good day, you can talk to her about almost anything, but I've always noticed she reserves a lot of that warmth for outsiders — my friends, my other aunties or uncles, people who don't even live with her. So sometimes I wonder how genuine it all actually is.

From an early age, I got to see who she really was, and honestly, she was scary. She flamed up easily, and I took my fair share of beatings as a child — with canes, with hands, you name it. I wasn't a perfect kid, so maybe I deserved it sometimes, but it was harsh. Whenever she was furious, she would shout at the absolute top of her voice. She's always pissed off about something, always murmuring, always asking questions, always doing something frantic. It's a bit too much for me, bro. So growing up, I had to legitimately learn how to tolerate — sometimes ignore — her. My auntie Rosemary used to tell me the reason she acts like that is because of what she's been through in life, work, marriage, and circumstances, so it's easy to understand why she's so easily triggered. But understanding it doesn't make living with it any easier.

It was actually through her anger that I first got suspicious about where I came from. Like I said, I wasn't a good kid, but anytime she was pissed off at me, she would resort to this specific insult in Ga: *"ony3 hi3 fl3fl3"* — insulting my mother. As a kid, I didn't know anything about adoption; I always thought they were my biological parents. But she kept saying that, and in my mind, the math wasn't adding up. I thought, *"If you're my mother, and you keep insulting 'my mother,' it means you are not the one."* It wasn't just once or twice. I always knew there was something off. Add to that the fact that there were zero baby pictures of me around the house — only pictures of other people I didn't know — and that's how the reality of my adoption slowly crept in.

I never felt any real connection with her. I don't think she ever really knew what was going on in my life. Maybe in high school she knew I was doing science, but that was basic. When I was in uni, she didn't really understand my tech stuff; she just liked asking me about it so she could brag to her friends whose kids were also studying tech at KNUST. The worst part was that she used to give my phone number to those random kids and tell me to call them and meet up. I absolutely hated it. I never, ever called them, and if they called me, I'd find an excuse. Why put me in those uncomfortable situations? We didn't even talk normally at home — it was always just a strained "hi" and "hello" — so why pretend we had this perfect, involved dynamic?

Compared to her siblings, she is wired so differently. My mother's side of the family — Mummy (Auntie Rosemary), Auntie Dinah, her stepbrother — are naturally jovial, outgoing people who love family gatherings and checking up on each other. You can tell they had fun growing up together.

When she first came back from the US, she was really into family stuff. I used to love following her to the marketplace where our family had shops; she'd buy me things, we'd visit relatives, and she was really open and well-known. But over the years, staying with my dad in Ghana changed her.

The lifestyle shift from the US to Ghana was rough. Over there, she drove her own car and moved freely. Here, they only had one or two cars at most, and eventually my dad sold hers. Suddenly, moving around became a problem. She had to take taxis or trotros just to get around. She started her bakery at my grandmother's place, which was an hour or two away, but after we got robbed, she had to move the business back to our house. Since we didn't have any house help or her workers sleeping over, I was the one helping her finish baking and catering. I'd lose so much sleep — working in the kitchen, going to school, coming back, and working again. I saw how hardworking she could be, and she was generous too, always giving money to younger people or external "sons and daughters" who needed help. Yet there I was in the house, feeling completely left out.

Her entire existence at home devolved into trying to please my dad out of pure fear. My dad drilled this toxic narrative into her head: the man is the head of the house, his word is final, wives should submit to their husbands, and divorce is evil — meaning you're stuck forever even if you hate it. She became terrified of offending him. If he was wrong, she couldn't say it. There were times the frustration boiled over into massive quarrels that got physical. I remember picking her up off the ground twice. Doors slamming, screaming — it was crazy. You could feel the discomfort in the house. They would wake up angry, try to avoid each other, and just look for trouble.

It wasn't a home.

The only time I ever saw them genuinely bond was when they were talking shit about me. Because they were always fighting, I was constantly caught in the middle. My dad would give me one set of instructions, and my mom would give me completely different ones. As a kid, I didn't know how to navigate it without offending one of them. Inevitably, I'd do something wrong, and suddenly they were a united front. I'd hear her on the phone complaining about me to people, and my parents would suddenly be cool with each other just so they could talk shit about me. Honestly, at one point, I even liked it that way. I figured if throwing me under the bus was what it took to ignite some communication in the house and stop the conflicting instructions, then fine.

Eight years ago, everything completely shattered when she got severely sick with diabetes. It got so bad she had to be put on drips in the hospital. That was when the truth came out: she had been managing this condition with a personal doctor since their time in the US and had hidden it from my dad all those years. When my dad found out, he didn't react with worry — he was purely furious that she had kept a secret. He decided he couldn't handle taking care of her, so we packed her clothes and dumped her at my grandmother's house for her family to deal with. My Auntie Rosemary was so disgusted by how my dad abandoned her sister that they had a massive fight. Because of that, the aunt who actually picked me up from the adoption agency hasn't spoken to my dad to this day.

Eventually, they brought my mom back home, and my dad handled her daily insulin shots and blood sugar checks. But you could see he was fed up. So the responsibility fell squarely on me. Before going to school every morning, I had to prick her finger, check her blood sugar, write it down, make sure she ate, give her the insulin injection, and pack her meds. It was exhausting, especially because she hated the needles and threw tantrums, but I did it because I thought I was helping.

The real battle, though, was her complete lack of self-control with her diet. When she was bedridden, she couldn't eat sugary or oily foods. But as she got slightly better and stopped needing the insulin, she went full normal mode. She started sneaking sweets, drinking soda, and even eating ice cream. My dad literally went to the local provision stores and ordered the shopkeepers not to sell anything sweet to his wife. She started hiding her junk food in plain sight.

During a church event, her sugar spiked so badly that she grew dizzy and almost fainted. My dad wasn't there, and she literally begged a close church friend not to tell him, terrified of the fallout. If you try to advise her now, she just flares up.

Her insecurities also ruined the help we did get. While she was sick, a relative who was a professional caterer took over the cooking for the household. She went to the market, cooked massive meals, and really helped us out. But the moment my mother started recovering, extreme jealousy took over. She couldn't stand someone else running her kitchen. She started spreading wild, toxic theories — claiming the woman was cooking poorly, trying to poison the food, and actively trying to lure my dad. It was entirely fabricated, but she made life so miserable that the woman eventually threw her hands up and quit.

So we slowly transitioned back to just the three of us.

My mom got better, but the house is still deeply uncomfortable. I've learned to just pretend and ignore her. Take a typical Saturday: I'm the only one cleaning the house, sweeping, mopping, doing the laundry, doing the bathrooms, mowing the lawn, and going to the market. I have pent-up anger about being treated like a houseboy with zero needs or feelings. While I'm doing all this, she'll try to make casual conversation with me. Honestly? I just ignore her. She can see I'm ignoring her, but she'll repeat her question three or four times before I finally reply. She has this bizarre sense of entitlement, expecting me to act like the perfect kid in the house while treating me like the help.

The one thing I am actually grateful to my mom for, though, happened recently. She loves cooking up theories and gossiping, and for a while she had been talking to a new family at church, quietly trying to set me up with their daughter. She was actively pushing for an arranged marriage. I had never, ever thought of an arranged marriage in my life, and I swore I'd never trust anything my parents picked for me. But when I finally saw the lady that first time on a Sunday, I was completely blown away. She was so beautiful. We exchanged contacts, and I couldn't stop thinking about her that night. We started talking, and she has such a good heart. I've grown to like her so much, and I really hope she feels the same way about me. If there's one arranged setup I'd accept in this world, it's this one. So for introducing her into my life, I am genuinely thankful to my mother.

Ultimately, my mom is a complex mixture of anger, love, entitlement, and optimistic giving. I'm not writing all this to shame her or make you hate her. As I always say, if you only know my parents from a distance, they'll show you exactly what they want you to see, and they seem like perfectly nice people. But living behind closed doors with her taught me how deeply environment, secrets, and fear can alter a human being. I've grown to like her in my own distant way, but I'm just trying to keep my peace, do my own thing, and survive.

But anyway, that's my mom for you. Looking back, it's crazy how long I lived in this house without connecting the dots. The signs were literally everywhere. I think for the next chapter, it's finally time to talk about the elephant in the room. I'll tell you how I actually found out I was adopted — and what exactly had to happen for that massive secret to spill out.
