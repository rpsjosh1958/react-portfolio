Adjusting to my new life with my adoptive parents came with its own rhythm. It was a comfortable routine, and my dad's career added a layer of excitement to it. He works on ships — he's a captain — and whenever vessels come into West Africa, he is called upon to inspect them, ensuring their journeys have been smooth and filing all the necessary maritime reports.

Whenever a ship docked in Takoradi, Tema, or Cape Coast, the three of us would travel down together. Even though I was still pretty quiet around them and didn't have that deep sense of familiarity yet — mostly listening to the two of them talk — I absolutely loved going on those ships because of him. Between those trips and our family beach days, we were building our own version of normal.

By 2011, when I was around eleven years old, my dad planned a summer vacation for us to the UK. It was an incredible experience. The three of us spent time in Huddersfield with my dad's cousin, visited Manchester, and traveled to Liverpool to see his cousin's daughter. From there, we went to my auntie's place in Colchester and finally headed into London to visit more family. Exploring the UK and seeing all those new places as a kid was pure fun.

But when we returned to Ghana, reality hit in a very unsettling way.

Because my parents both worked, I was usually the first one to get home from school. They had a system where they would leave the house key with the shopkeepers at the stores right in front of our house. I'd grab the key, let myself in, and wait for them to get home.

One afternoon, I walked up to the house and noticed the front gate was wide open. Walking inside, the front door was completely open too. The fans were spinning, the lights were on, but the house had been entirely hollowed out. The TV was gone. The speakers were gone. Rooms had been ransacked, and our heavy metal slider doors, which sat just past the glass doors, were visibly tampered with and broken.

I stood there at ten or eleven years old, completely confused and trying to process what I was looking at. I ran out to the front stores to ask if anyone had seen anything.

The response blew my mind. The shopkeepers told me that a car had parked outside earlier, and people had spent the afternoon casually packing everything out of the house. One of the thieves even had the absolute audacity to walk over to the store and buy a drink right in front of them. Because they weren't hiding anything, it looked like they were working for us.

Panicked, I had them call my mother and dad immediately. When they rushed home, the reality set in: we had been completely robbed. It was a hectic, chaotic evening, and for some reason, we all ended up sleeping together in the main hall that night.

The crazy part is that we eventually found out exactly who did it, but he was long gone, and we had no clue where he went. The security breach changed everything. My parents couldn't risk leaving the house empty during the day anymore, and that was the turning point that forced my mother to move her entire bakery business directly into the house.
